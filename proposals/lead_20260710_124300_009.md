# Project Proposal — Structural Drawings

## Project Understanding
You need assistance with: 
**PRD: the example's control panel — a feature list, a detail pane, and a demo that opens on a chosen state**

*Project description:*
## Problem Statement

A reader opens the example to find out what `flutter_folderview` can do. What they get is a 350px column holding nine collapsed drawers, above a tree.

Nothing on that screen says what the package does. The drawer titles — `Line`, `Folder`, `Parent`, `Child`, `Tooltip`, `Layout` — name the things a setting is *about*, not the things a setting *changes*. To find out whether the package has row-wide tooltips, a reader must guess that they live under `Tooltip`, open it, and scroll past thirty-four controls.

The state the demo opens in was never chosen. The three per-Tier label tooltips default on; the row card defaults off. So the feature this repo spent five issues on (#44, #48, #50, #51, #52) is invisible until a reader finds the switch that reveals it. Meanwhile `example/pubspec.yaml` advertises the demo as covering "every theme knob, both tooltips, and the scale gesture". Two thirds of that is false: `RowTooltipTheme` has eighteen public fields and the demo exposes none of them, hardcoding a single `waitDuration` in the page; and only one of the two tooltips is on when the app starts.

The drawer that does open is worse than the ones that don't. `Tooltip` holds roughly thirty-four controls — ten switches, sixteen sliders, four colour rows, four choice-chip groups — under a heading that calls sixteen of them "Common Settings". They are not common. They fill `NodeTooltipTheme` three times, once per Tier, and the row card reads none of them.

So the panel fails a reader twice. At rest it hides what exists. Opened, it presents an undifferentiated list with no statement of what owns what, what depends on what, or what changes what.

## Solution

Replace the drawers with a two-part panel: a narrow list of the demo's features, always visible, and a detail pane that draws one feature at a time.

The list is the map. Every feature appears on it, grouped by what a reader is trying to do, showing whether the feature is on and how many options it owns. Nothing is hidden behind a click. A feature that can be switched off carries a dot that toggles it; a feature that is always live carries no dot and reads as a heading over settings — the absence is meaningful and the list says so.

The pane is the workbench. Selecting a feature draws its switch and its options, and nothing else. A feature that is off can still be opened: its options appear, unreachable, naming the switch that would enable them, because a reader deserves to know the difference between a setting that does not exist and one that cannot currently be used.

Above both sits a preset bar. Presets are named for the interaction they demonstrate, not for the switches they flip. `Row card over a long label` earns its name because it answers which tooltip wins under the pointer; `Row card` does not, because a switch already says that. The demo opens on a named preset chosen deliberately rather than on whatever the field defaults happened to accumulate.

Under all of it, the panel's contents stop being a pile of widgets and become data: groups hold features, a feature owns the options that only mean something once it is on, and a feature names the other features it changes. Tests hold that description honest — add a field and forget to describe it, and the suite goes red.

## Implementation Decisions

**The panel's contents become data.** A group holds features; a feature holds a title, an optional switch, the options that only mean something once it is on, and the interactions it has with other features; an interaction names the other feature, states the effect, and cites its evidence. This mirrors the description that `flutter_table_plus` arrived at, and the mirroring is deliberate: it is the same author, the same problem, and the shape has already survived contact with a rendering rewrite there.

**A feature's switch is a boolean settings field, or nothing.** A feature without a switch is a heading over settings that are always live. Two of our settings look like switches and are not: `LineStyle.none` is an option *value*, and the guard on the tooltip's border colour is `borderWidth > 0`. Neither becomes a feature switch, and the description gains no predicate machinery to express them. `Line` is a switchless heading whose style option happens to include `none`; the border colour's dependency on a non-zero width stays a pane-local rendering concern. This keeps the invariant "a switch names a real boolean field" enforceable by a test.

**Groups are cut by what a setting changes, not by what it is about.** The nine drawers cut across features: the row card's switch and the three label-tooltip switches share the `Tooltip` drawer despite feeding different theme objects, while sixteen sliders sit under a "Common Settings" heading and reach only the label tooltips. The re-cut separates the row card from the label tooltips, and the sixteen shared sliders belong to the label tooltips alone.

**`RowTooltipTheme` gets exposed.** The row card is a feature with a switch and, today, zero options, because the demo hardcodes a `waitDuration` and ignores the theme's other seventeen public fields. An empty detail pane would be an honest report of that gap, but the gap should be closed instead: `example/pubspec.yaml` promises "every theme knob, both tooltips", and the row card is the feature this repo spent five issues on. Exposing it also gives the pane something to draw, and gives the reader a way to compare the two tooltip surfaces under the same settings.

**The demo's opening state is chosen, not inherited.** Today three label tooltips are on and the row card is off, an arrangement nobody decided. The demo opens on a named preset. Which preset is a decision the preset slice makes deliberately, with the argument recorded: a bare tree teaches, because every later addition has a zero to be measured against, while the row card's invisibility at startup is the concrete defect a non-bare opening state would keep hiding.

**Presets are pure functions over the demo's view model.** A preset is a set of field assignments applied via `copyWith`; it needs neither the description nor the new panel to exist, which is why it is unblocked and goes first. A preset named for an interaction must also arrange the data that makes the interaction observable — a preset about a long label must produce a long label.

**The panel is rebuilt expand-then-contract.** The feature list is built beside the existing drawers, which keep working. The drawers are deleted only in a later change, once the detail pane draws everything they drew. No single change leaves the demo unusable, and the deletion has a working reference to be checked against. This is the sequencing `flutter_table_plus` adopted after four merged pull requests changed nothing a reader could see; the same trap is available here, and the presets-first ordering is the same escape.

**Interactions are drawn from ADRs and library control flow, never from panel structure.** Candidate citations already exist: ADR-0004 (tooltips are chrome and excluded from Scale), ADR-0003 (Selection is bound to the Child Tier), ADR-0002 (the caller owns Expansion and Selection, so the demo mutates them), ADR-0005 (the three Tier themes duplicate deliberately), and the negative result in `#51` that a row card cannot steal a tap because hit testing is depth-first. A citation that points at the panel's own layout is forbidden: the panel is exactly what this work rearranges, and the sibling repo watched such a citation rot within a day.

**Vocabulary.** The list shows features, not sections. A `Node` is never called a row; a row is a rendered line. The three Tiers are Folder, Parent and Child. `Scale`, `View Mode`, `Expansion` and `Selection` keep their glossary meanings.

**The panel's dartdoc is corrected.** It calls itself "the demo's right-hand control panel" while sitting as the first child of the page's `Row`. It is on the left.

## Testing Decisions

A good test here asserts what a reader would see, and fails when the thing it describes stops being true. It does not name a private widget, does not count anonymous controls it cannot identify, and does not assert the absence of something by looking for a key that would also be absent for the wrong reason — this repo has already shipped a test that passed because a card key was missing while the wrapper it was meant to catch was present (`#48`).

**Two seams, and one of them is not a widget.**

The first is pure. The description of the panel and the application of a preset are data and a function over the demo's view model; both are tested with plain `test()` and no `pumpWidget`. Two invariants live here and are the point of describing anything as data at all: every settings field appears in the description exactly once, and every interaction names a feature that exists. A third asserts that every interaction carries a citation — a test can insist the citation is present, it cannot confirm the cited code says what the interaction claims, and so confirming them is review work, not test work. Preset application is asserted here too: applying `Bare` to the default view model yields a view model with every switch off.

The second seam is `MyApp`, pumped whole. `example/test/widget_test.dart` already does this and is the prior art; it boots the demo and asserts a `FolderView` renders a seeded Node's label. Everything visible goes through it: the list renders every described feature under its group, a dot toggles its feature and the tree changes accordingly, clicking a name selects without toggling, the pane draws exactly the controls the description gives the selected feature and nothing belonging to another, an off feature's options render disabled and name their switch, search narrows the list and restores it when cleared.

No new widget seam is opened. Pumping a panel widget in isolation would size it in a `SizedBox` rather than in the page's 350px column, and this repo has already learned that a `Flexible` measured inside an artificial box reports an ellipsized label where the real view reports none (`#52`). The panel's width is a property of the page, so the page is what gets pumped.

**The characterisation test that exists will need to change, and that is correct.** `example/test/widget_test.dart` asserts the demo boots and a Node's label renders. That survives. Any assertion that counts every control on screen cannot survive a detail pane, because the pane draws one feature at a time. The property to preserve is counting without naming: the selected feature shows exactly the controls the description gives it — its switch, its options, and nothing else. Still counted, still not named, so the next rearrangement has something to fail against.

**Overflow is asserted, not assumed.** The feature list is narrow, and the widget-test font draws every glyph as a square of the font size, so a name that fits on screen can overflow under test. `flutter_table_plus` fixed this twice in the same panel. Our example has no prior art for it; each slice that draws the list or the pane asserts no overflow.

**Mutation-check before claiming a test works.** Each pull request records which mutation was introduced and what died. A test that asserts an absence, and a test whose fixture gives the same key to more than one widget, both pass for the wrong reason until a mutation proves otherwise. Commit before mutating: `git restore` returns a file to `HEAD`, not to an uncommitted fix.

**CI runs the example's tests.** The example job in `.github/workflows/ci.yml` runs `flutter pub get`, `dart format --set-exit-if-changed`, `flutter analyze` and `flutter test`. Formatting is a separate gate from analysis and fails independently. Integration tests under `example/integration_test` are not run by CI and are not a gate for this work.

## Out of Scope

The library itself. Nothing under `lib/` changes: no new public API, no theme field, no widget. Exposing `RowTooltipTheme`'s knobs means the demo passes fields it already accepts.

The `FolderView` half of the screen, apart from what a preset sets and what a control changes. No new demo pages, no home menu, no focused single-feature demos. `flutter_table_plus` has those; whether this repo wants them is a separate question and a separate issue.

Integration tests. The panel rework is asserted through `example/test`, which CI runs. The two files under `example/integration_test` drive a real desktop app, are not run by CI, and are not extended here.

Any redesign of the demo's data generator beyond listing it as a switchless feature.

The correctness of the tooltip behaviour itself. If a stated interaction turns out to be false when its citation is read, the fix is to correct the statement, and to open an issue for the behaviour if the behaviour is what is wrong.

## Further Notes

**What has not been verified.** No one has run this app during the work that produced this document. The control counts are from static reads and are lower bounds — an early count of 77 missed the choice-chip groups entirely, because they matched no `slider(` or `Switch` pattern. Pixel density, scroll length, and how the seven colour swatches wrap at 350px are unmeasured. Nothing in this document asserts how the panel *feels*; the claims are about what exists, what defaults to what, and what feeds what.

**Measure, do not look.** The temptation is to answer the unmeasured questions by running the app and eyeballing it. For a claim a test can hold, that is the wrong instrument: an eyeballed confirmation leaves no artefact, so reverting the code it confirmed turns nothing red. #52 is the case that looks like an argument for eyeballing and is not — a label reads as ellipsized inside a fixed-width box and unellipsized inside a `FolderView`, the same code supporting opposite conclusions depending on where it is measured. The lesson is to measure in the right place, with a probe test that prints the rect, and then to pin the number in an assertion. Probes are thrown away; the numbers go on the issue. This is how #42 found a 270px gap between two tooltip anchors and how #47 found that a label it had called "ellipsized" had `didExceedMaxLines == false`.

Running the app is still worth doing, for the residue a test cannot judge — whether the panel feels crowded, whether the swatches wrap into something ugly. That residue does not become an acceptance criterion, because it cannot go red.

**Why presets come first.** The sibling repo sliced this work as control-id, then settings-as-data, then a rendering rewrite, then dependency gating, and merged four pull requests before noticing the screen had barely changed. It re-cut with presets first, blocked by nothing, on the reasoning that a preset never needed any of that. The same ordering applies here and for a sharper reason: our opening state hides the row card, and a preset fixes that in one change.

**The diagnosis does not transfer, only the remedy.** The sibling's complaint was a wall of sixty-eight toggles standing in one column. Ours is the inverse at rest — nine closed drawers, nothing discoverable — and becomes a wall only once a drawer opens. Master-detail happens to fix both. This document adopts the remedy, not the reasoning, and the distinction matters if a later reader wonders why our list is short where theirs is long.

**Two errors made while drafting, recorded so they are not repeated.** First, the sibling's features were assumed to all carry switches, from reading its issue prose; its `switchId` is nullable by design and five of twenty-one features have none. Second, our own switch count was taken over drawers rather than features, which reported two where a re-cut by feature reports about ten. Both errors came from reading summaries instead of source. The counts in this document come from the source.

**`CLAUDE.md` is stale on one point.** It states that the example CI job does not run `flutter test`. It does, since the `flutter create` counter test was replaced. The claim should be corrected when this work touches the example's test surface.
