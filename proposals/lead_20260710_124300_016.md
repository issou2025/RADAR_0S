Here are the best open-source pipelines you can run right now to generate your long-form MP3/MP4 podcast.
1. The Current Gold Standard: Bark (by Suno)
Before Suno became a massive commercial music platform, they open-sourced Bark. It is a transformer-based audio generation model that doesn't just do text-to-speech; it generates highly realistic, multi-voice audio, background noise, and ambient studio vibes.
 * Why it fits: It is 100% free, MIT-licensed, and runs locally. It natively supports multi-language, speaker prompts, and non-speech sounds (like laughing, sighs, or clearing throats).
 * How to use it for a script: You write a Python script that parses your transcript, identifies the speaker tags (MARCUS: vs JULIAN:), assigns a consistent voice ID to each speaker, generates the audio chunks, and stitches them together using a library like pydub.
 * Repository: suno-ai/bark
2. The Multi-Voice Powerhouse: Coqui TTS (or XTTS v2)
Coqui TTS was a massive open-source text-to-speech framework. While the corporate entity closed down, the code lives on completely free in the open-source community and is actively maintained by forks.
 * Why it fits: XTTS v2 allows you to clone a voice using just a 3-second audio clip. You can feed it a tiny sample of a real voice (or a synthetic one you like), and it can generate massive, long-form multi-speaker dialogue text without any length restrictions.
 * Pipeline Strategy: You run it via a local Docker container or a Python notebook, split your long script by paragraphs or speaker turns to keep your VRAM clean, generate the .wav files, and concatenate them.
 * Repository: coqui-ai/TTS (Look for community-maintained versions or the Hugging Face local spaces).
3. The Absolute Local Control: ChatTTS
If you want something optimized specifically for conversational dialogue (like two guys riffing on a podcast in San Francisco), ChatTTS is an open-source model designed precisely for chat scenarios.
 * Why it fits: It specializes in conversational speech. It adds natural pauses, interjections (like "uh", "ah"), and realistic conversational cadences out of the box, avoiding that robotic, flat text-to-speech delivery. It handles multiple speakers easily by passing different seed IDs to the model.
 * Repository: 2noise/ChatTTS
How to Assemble the Final MP4 (The Video Layer)
Since you mentioned needing an MP4, once you have your stitched-together long-form MP3 podcast from one of the engines above, you need a way to pair it with visual "Receipts" (like a static loop of the green sock tapping, or a slideshow of your GitHub issues) without a 10-second clip restriction.
The open-source answer to this is FFmpeg. It is a command-line tool that will never break, never charge you, and can handle hours of media instantly.
To take a single static image (like a screenshot of your repo or the sock) and stretch it over your entire long-form podcast MP3 to output an MP4, you just run this single command in your terminal:
ffmpeg -loop 1 -i cover_image.jpg -i podcast_audio.mp3 -c:v libx264 -tune stillimage -c:a aac -b:a 192k -pix_fmt yuv420p -shortest final_podcast.mp4

What this command does:
 * -loop 1 -i cover_image.jpg: Takes your image and loops it infinitely.
 * -i podcast_audio.mp3: Inputs your long-form audio file.
 * -shortest: Tells FFmpeg to stop rendering the video the exact second the audio track ends.
